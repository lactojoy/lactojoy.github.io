DiTTo-TTS:

Efficient and Scalable Text-to-Speech with Diffusion Transformer

|OpenReview|

OpenReview

Abstract. Diffusion models have demonstrated remarkable success in images, videos, and audio generation. However, applying these models to text-to-speech (TTS) poses challenges due to the need for precise synchronization between text and generated speech. This work presents a latent diffusion model specifically tailored for TTS, integrating pre-trained text and speech encoders without relying on domain-specific modeling. One key feature of this approach is a module that predicts the total length of the generated speech from the given text. Our model leverages the Diffusion Transformer (DiT) and undergoes a network architecture ablation to optimize it for TTS applications. We scale the training dataset to 82K hours and the model size to 790M parameters. Our experiments show that the proposed model yields performance that is superior or comparable to state-of-the-art TTS models in terms of naturalness, intelligibility, and speaker similarity. Additionally, our model exhibits robust scalability with increasing data and model size.

Model Overview

The overview of DiTTo-TTS architecture. The DiT based LDM is trained to denoise by using noisy and contextual data, employing span masking. The speech length predictor is based on causal tranformer blocks. Both DiT blocks and the speech length predictor employ cross-attention to condition on text representations from a text encoder. Additionally, DiT blocks utilize AdaLN with the aggregated text embedding.

Zero-Shot TTS (LibriSpeech)

DiTTo-TTS utilizes in-context learning to generate speech from a reference audio paired with its corresponding text. In our representations, prompts are indicated in blue while the produced span audio is shown in green. The synthesized speech mirrors the reference audio in terms of voice, background noise, and speech style. When segmenting prompts into 3-second intervals, it's possible that the audio may not always conclude with silence, occasionally resulting in the truncation of words. For more in-depth information, refer to Section 4.2 in our paper.
